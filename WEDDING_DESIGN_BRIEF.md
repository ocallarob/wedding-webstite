## 02 · Typography

### Digital (website)
- **Headings / Display:** Cormorant Garamond — weights 300, 400, 500; light italic for Irish phrases and pull quotes
- **Body / Navigation / Labels:** Jost — weights 300, 400, 500

### Print (menus, programmes, stationery)
- **Headings / Display:** Cormorant Garamond — same as digital
- **Body:** EB Garamond (preferred over Jost for print — keeps everything in the serif family, gives a more heirloom feel)

### Type scale rules
- Hero / couple names: Cormorant Garamond 300, large, wide letter-spacing (~0.1em)
- Section headings: Cormorant Garamond 300–400, letter-spacing ~0.05em
- Section eyebrows / labels: Jost 400–500, ALL CAPS, letter-spacing ~0.2–0.3em, Fermanagh Mauve colour
- Body text: Jost or EB Garamond 300–400, normal case, relaxed line-height (~1.7)
- Irish language phrases: Cormorant Garamond 300 italic, Fermanagh Mauve or Blush Rose colour
- Card timestamps / accent numbers: Cormorant Garamond, Fermanagh Mauve colour

## 03 · Motifs & Repeating Visual Elements

These five elements should appear consistently across every material — digital and print. They are the visual language of the wedding.

### 1. Castle Silhouettes (primary motif)
- **Menlo Castle** (Galway — Rob's home) and **Devenish Island Round Tower** (Fermanagh — Alannah's home)
- Style: simplified single-weight outline illustrations, no fill, transparent background
- Used as: watermarks on invitation interior, programme cover illustration, envelope liner, hero section watermark on website
- Delivery format: SVG with clean paths; high-res PNG at 300dpi minimum
- These two buildings are always used as a pair — they represent the dual heritage story of the wedding

### 2. Lough Water Line (section divider)
- A gentle double-wave horizontal line representing Lough Erne
- Two parallel curves, hand-drawn in character
- Used wherever a section break or divider is needed — replaces a standard horizontal rule
- Colour: Lough Gold (`#d4c5a9`) or Fermanagh Mauve (`#9c7a8c`) at 0.5–1pt stroke weight

### 3. Diamond Gem Accent (punctuation motif)
- A small rotated square (◇) used between names, dates, menu course separators, and monograms
- Example usage: `A ◇ R`, `Ceremony · ◇ · Reception`
- Scales from 8px inline to large decorative centrepiece
- Colour: Lough Gold or Fermanagh Mauve

### 4. Botanical Sprigs (corner adornment)
- Loose, single-line botanical illustrations — Irish wildflowers, fern fronds, soft floral sprigs
- Style: Bridgerton-inspired, delicate, minimal — not heavy or overwrought
- Used as: corner adornments on invitations and programmes, envelope flap interior decoration
- Colour: Connaught Sage (`#8fa888`) on ivory stock; can include Blush Rose flower accents

### 5. Irish Language Phrases (textual motif)
- One short Irish phrase per material, used as a footer or closing line
- Rendering: Cormorant Garamond 300 italic, Fermanagh Mauve or Blush Rose
- **Approved phrases:**
  - `Le grá agus le háthas` — With love and joy (primary phrase, used most widely)
  - `Fáilte romhaibh` — Welcome (used on signage and menus)
  - `Go raibh maith agaibh` — Thank you (used on thank-you cards)

## 04 · Mood & Aesthetic Direction

### In one sentence
Bridgerton brought to Fermanagh — romantic, cinematic, grounded in real Irish landscape.

### Style keywords
Romantic · Elegant · Timeless · Soft · Editorial · Modern Regency · Irish Heritage

### Aesthetic references
- **Era feel:** Bridgerton (Regency-inspired romance, but updated and modern — not costume-heavy)
- **Photography style:** Editorial film photography — soft focus, warm tones, unhurried; not Instagram-filtered
- **Landscape:** Late August Irish lough shore — golden diffuse light, emerald hills, mist on still water
- **Material feel:** Textured linen, ivory card stock, gold foil, uncoated paper

### What to avoid
- Bright white, stark modern minimalism, cold or cool colour tones
- Heavy ornate Victorian decoration — keep it refined, not fussy
- Trendy design patterns (neon, brutalism, high-contrast sans-serif only)
- Anything that reads as generic or template-like

### Mood description (for AI image prompting)
> "A late August afternoon on the shores of an Irish lough — the light is golden and diffuse, warm ivory and dusty rose and sage green, the air soft with the end of summer. The setting is a grand lakeside resort hotel; the occasion is intimate despite its scale. The mood is Bridgerton brought to Fermanagh: romantic and cinematic, but grounded in real Irish landscape — ancient stone, still water, wildflowers in the hedgerows."

> "Visuals should feel timeless rather than trendy — editorial film photography, not Instagram filters. Soft focus, warm tones, unhurried. Irish heritage details (round towers, castle ruins, textured linen, gold calligraphy) balance the contemporary elegance of the venue."

## 05 · Tone of Voice

Applies to: website copy, invitations, menus, programmes, signage, RSVP communications, thank-you cards.

| Dimension | Direction |
|---|---|
| Register | Warm formal — formal enough to feel considered, warm enough to feel personal |
| Personality | Quietly witty, never try-hard — light humour is welcome, especially on menus and FAQ; primary voice is gracious not comedic |
| Pace | Unhurried — never clipped or corporate; give details room to breathe |
| Irish identity | Present, not performative — woven in lightly via place names and phrases, not leaned on heavily |

### Write like this
- *"We've chosen five courses that celebrate the very best of Fermanagh — and one or two things that simply couldn't wait."*
- *"If you're travelling from further afield, Enniskillen is well worth arriving a day early for. We're a little biased."*
- *"We're so excited to have you as part of our day."*

### Not like this
- *"Please find below our wedding menu. Dietary requirements should be noted on your RSVP form."*
- *"Guests are advised to arrange accommodation in advance due to limited availability."*

### Rules
- No bullet points in romantic or emotional copy — write in sentences
- Avoid passive voice
- Don't over-explain logistics — keep practical information brief and warm
- Always use both names together when addressing the couple: *Rob & Alannah*, never just one name alone
